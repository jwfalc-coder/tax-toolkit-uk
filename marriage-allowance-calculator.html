Marriage Allowance Calculator UK 2025/26

Marriage Allowance lets one spouse or civil partner transfer 10% of their personal allowance to the other, saving up to £252 in tax per year. It applies where one partner earns below the personal allowance threshold and the other is a basic rate taxpayer. It sounds simple, but many eligible couples have never claimed it, and HMRC allows backdated claims for up to four previous tax years, meaning unclaimed allowances could be worth over £1,000 as a lump sum. Explained

How Marriage Allowance works

Marriage Allowance was introduced in 2015 to provide a modest tax break to couples where one partner does not use their full personal allowance. The logic is simple: if one person is not earning enough to use all of their £12,570 allowance, transferring part of it to their spouse lets the household pay less tax overall.

1

Who is eligible

To qualify, you must be married or in a civil partnership (cohabiting couples do not qualify). The lower earner must have income below the personal allowance of £12,570. The higher earner must be a basic rate taxpayer, their income must be between £12,571 and £50,270. Neither partner can be a higher or additional rate taxpayer.

2

What is transferred

The lower earner transfers exactly 10% of their personal allowance to their partner. For 2025/26 this is £1,257 (10% of £12,570). The lower earner's personal allowance reduces to £11,313 and the higher earner's increases to £13,827. The higher earner then pays 20% less tax on the additional £1,257 of income that is now covered by their increased allowance, saving £252.

3

How to apply

The lower earner makes the application through HMRC's online service at gov.uk/marriage-allowance. You need both partners' National Insurance numbers. Once approved, the higher earner's tax code is updated to reflect the additional allowance. The application can also include backdated claims for up to four previous tax years.

4

Backdated claims

If you were eligible in previous years but never claimed, you can backdate by up to four years. Claims covering 2022/23, 2023/24, 2024/25, and 2025/26 could total £1,008 paid as a lump sum. The lower earner still applies, and HMRC will adjust both partners' records accordingly.

Death of a spouse: Marriage Allowance can be backdated following the death of a spouse, even where the surviving partner is now a higher rate taxpayer. HMRC allows claims for years in which both conditions were met, regardless of later changes in circumstance.

Yes, they are two completely different reliefs. Marriage Allowance (the transfer of 10% of the personal allowance) is available to all eligible married couples and civil partners regardless of age.
